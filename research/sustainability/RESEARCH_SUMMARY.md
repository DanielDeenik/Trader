### 6. ESG SCORING HUMAN JUDGMENT GATES (Information Arbitrage Parallel)

**Key Finding**: ESG scoring requires subjective human judgment similar to security analysis mosaic work.

**Evidence**:
- 60% of ESG scores based on aspirational promises (not actual performance)
- <45% of score variation attributable to company disclosure (rest = methodology design)
- Crisil ESG Ratings: 500+ KPIs, 65+ sectors, committee-based review process
- BNP Paribas: Integrates third-party data + in-house research + investment team interaction
- Sustainalytics: 1,800+ data points per company, analyst discretion on weighting

**Judgment Gates** (Arbitrage Opportunities):
1. **Data Collection**: Which data points material? Analysts disagree.
2. **Aspiration vs. Performance**: SBT announcement vs. actual emissions reduction progress
3. **Industry Materiality**: ESG KPI relevance differs by sector (carbon for energy, water for packaged goods)
4. **Peer Benchmarking**: Judging trajectory vs. peer set (5-year view? 10-year? 2050?)
5. **Timing**: When does improved performance get reflected in scores? (Typically 6-18 month lag)

**Arbitrage Implication**: Companies with improving actual ESG fundamentals but lagging consensus scores = repricing opportunity. Timing lag creates entry window for mosaic researchers to detect before consensus.

## CONCLUSION

The private sustainability ecosystem in early 2026 exhibits classic information arbitrage characteristics:

1. **Regulatory predictability** (CSRD phases, DoD mandates) creates visible demand curves
2. **Methodological fragmentation** (ESG rating divergence) enables analysis advantage
3. **Timing lags** (6-18 months between performance improvement and score updates) create repricing windows
4. **Capital constraints** (limited high-quality carbon credits, ESG talent shortage) create supply/demand imbalances
5. **Private market gap** (1,000s of companies with zero institutional ESG coverage) enables asymmetric research

**The core arbitrage**: Build proprietary mosaic intelligence from non-material public signals (regulatory timelines, corporate commitments, hiring patterns, capital flows, ESG divergence) to identify repricing catalysts ahead of institutional consensus.

The human judgment requirement in ESG scoring is not a limitation—it's the structural feature enabling information arbitrage comparable to traditional security analysis.
